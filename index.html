Why projections are reliable

Storage $/GB fell for decades, but consumer NVMe spiked ~2× in 2025–2026 (AI datacenter demand, NAND shortage — TrendForce, Tom's Hardware). The model anchors 2026 at ~$130/TB (~$260/2 TB) and assumes a gradual recovery, then long-run deflation resumes.

Mini-PCs, ARM boards, and efficient SoCs compound cheaper + lower watts. Residential electricity rose ~55% nominally 2009–2026 (EIA); baseline projects modest continued increases. Internet is flat-to-down in real terms per USTelecom BPI while nominal plan costs track typical household broadband.

Even conservative (pessimistic) assumptions show the node stays trivially affordable forever — chain growth is linear, tech deflation is exponential.

Current Year Breakdown

Archival Node

  • Runs the full 750+ GB blockchain plus indexes.
  • Requires 2 TB storage — today that means spending roughly $450–$550 on an N100 mini PC plus 2 TB SSD. Longer term, as drive prices normalize, this cost should come down.
  • Best if you want to run an Electrum server or scan many different wallets quickly. Fast and convenient, but more expensive.

Pruned Node

  • Only keeps recent blocks — total size around 15–20 GB.
  • Can usually run on your existing laptop or desktop at almost no extra cost.
  • Wallet scanning is slower, and switching to a new wallet often requires a full reindex, which is time-consuming.

Simple trade-off: pay more for convenience and flexibility, or keep it cheap and simple if you're only using one or two wallets regularly.

Why It Got Cheaper — FAQ
2009–2011 spare-PC era: Early nodes reused desktops you already owned — we show ~$50 marginal kit cost, not a $400 laptop purchase. Dedicated hardware pricing kicks in from 2012 as the chain grew.
Storage price history: Bulk HDD $/TB fell from ~$110 (2009) to ~$10 (2026). Consumer NVMe was ~$50–70/TB through 2024–2025, then spiked to ~$130/TB in 2026 — the era shown for new archival builds (auto mode uses SSD from 2018+).
Efficient hardware: Raspberry Pi 4/5 runs Bitcoin Core with 4–8 GB RAM, replacing $500+ desktops from 2009–2012.
Bandwidth abundance: Unlimited home internet became standard; node traffic is a tiny fraction of monthly caps after initial sync.
OpEx assumptions (USA): Internet plan cost follows USTelecom Broadband Pricing Index anchors — ~$38/mo entry broadband (2009), ~$66 (2015 popular tier), ~$52 (2020), ~$73 (2024 BPI-Speed 100–940 Mbps). Default attributes 15% of the plan to the node. Electricity uses EIA residential averages: 11.5¢/kWh (2009) → 17.8¢/kWh (2026), with ~15 W default draw (Pi/mini-PC; raise for a desktop).
Software optimization: Compact blocks, assumevalid, and better I/O let Core run on modest hardware while still validating the full chain.
The 2026 NAND spike: Consumer 2 TB NVMe jumped from ~$120–150 (2025 lows) to ~$250–300+ in 2026 as AI datacenters absorbed NAND supply. A 2 TB archival build today is mostly the drive, not the mini-PC.
The catch: Absolute chain size still grows ~50–65 GB/year. Archival nodes always need more disk — historically $/TB fell faster than the chain grew; the 2026 spike is a temporary reversal.
Pruned vs archival storage: Pruned nodes validate the full chain but discard old block files. Steady-state disk grew with the chainstate (UTXO set): <1 GB (2010–12), ~2–3 GB (2015), ~5 GB (2017–18), ~8–10 GB (2020–22), ~15–20 GB today (2025–26, ~11 GB chainstate + headroom) — still far below ~870 GB archival. First sync still downloads the full chain (~740 GB bandwidth) either way.
Pruned cost model: Pruned mode assumes Core on a laptop/desktop you already own. One-time setup is $0 by default — you use existing free disk space (~18 GB today), not a new Pi or USB drive. The real pruned cost is annual OpEx (electricity + internet share). Toggle “USB/SD reference” in Assumptions if your disk is full and you’d buy a small stick.
2031–2040 (projected): Hardware class stays similar (efficient mini PC / ARM, 16 GB RAM) — bigger change is buying 4–8 TB NVMe instead of 2–4 TB. Baseline assumes NAND supply recovers and $/TB resumes deflation.
